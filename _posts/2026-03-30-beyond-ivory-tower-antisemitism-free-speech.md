On October 7, 2023, Hamas attacked Israel, killing over 1,200 people. Within days, protests erupted on American university campuses -- not against Hamas, but against Israel. Slogans that faculty and administrators struggled to categorize as either legitimate political speech or antisemitic incitement appeared on quads and in classrooms across the country.

In *Beyond the Ivory Tower*, published as a research paper by the Academic Engagement Network, Seth C. Oranburg examines the question that universities have found hardest to answer: is anti-Zionism antisemitism?

## The legal landscape

The question is not merely philosophical. Title VI of the Civil Rights Act prohibits discrimination on the basis of race, color, and national origin in federally funded programs. If anti-Zionist speech creates a hostile environment for Jewish students based on their national origin or ethnicity, universities have a legal obligation to respond. If it is protected political expression, they have a legal obligation not to.

Oranburg surveys the legal framework -- Title VI, free speech doctrine, hate crime statutes -- and finds that existing law provides no clear answer. The courts have not definitively resolved whether anti-Zionism directed at Jewish students constitutes national-origin harassment or protected political advocacy. Universities are left to draw the line themselves, with little guidance and enormous pressure from all sides.

## Firsthand observation

The article's second section departs from doctrinal analysis. Oranburg traveled to Israel after October 7 and visited the sites of the attacks -- Kibbutz Nir Oz, the Nova Festival grounds. He recounts what he saw in direct, first-person prose: the physical evidence of the assault, the testimonies of survivors, the scale of destruction.

This section does not argue a legal point. It establishes a factual baseline. Oranburg's implicit argument is that the campus debate about October 7 has proceeded in an information vacuum -- that many of the people making the loudest claims about the conflict have not reckoned with what actually happened.

## A liberal-realist framework

The article's final section proposes what Oranburg calls a liberal-realist framework for institutional responses. The framework draws on virtue ethics rather than speech codes. Instead of trying to define which slogans are permissible and which are not -- a line-drawing exercise that inevitably fails -- universities should cultivate institutional cultures of intellectual honesty, mutual respect, and engagement with evidence.

This is not a call for neutrality. Oranburg argues that institutions can and should be clear about their values -- including the value of truthful inquiry -- without policing specific viewpoints. The distinction is between institutional integrity (which requires moral clarity) and viewpoint regulation (which violates academic freedom).
