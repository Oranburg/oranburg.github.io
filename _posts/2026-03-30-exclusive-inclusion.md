After October 7, 2023, university presidents told Congress they could not guarantee the safety of Jewish students. Federal officials launched Title VI investigations into more than sixty institutions. Nine-figure settlements followed. National surveys confirmed what affected students already knew: 57.3% of Jewish students reported direct experiences of antisemitism, 63.4% reported bystander exposure, and most lacked confidence that their universities would respond.

These findings documented a governance failing, not a cultural one. They identified the symptom without diagnosing the disease.

## The disease is structural

Encampments, exclusion zones, faculty boycotts, and the systematic intimidation of Jewish students are not governance anomalies. They are governance *outputs* — predictable products of institutional architecture that creates an accountability void at the center of university decision-making. At multiple University of California campuses, departmental units used delegated authority to advance coordinated campaigns that fueled exclusion, and the Board of Regents had no governance mechanism to intervene. Faculty boycott endorsers held academic leadership positions at UCLA, UC Berkeley, and UC Santa Cruz, shaping curricula and speaker selection through captured governance structures.

I call this condition *exclusive inclusion*: the governance state in which an institution claims to protect all members while structurally ensuring that some are unprotected. The mechanism has three architectural features: self-perpetuating boards eliminate electoral accountability, hollow fiduciary duties eliminate legal accountability, and the absence of stakeholder standing eliminates affected-party accountability. The resulting void makes systematic exclusion structurally likely and, when it occurs, structurally difficult to remedy.

## Not just antisemitism

This is the critical point, and the one most often missed in public debate: the mechanism is not specific to any single identity group. The same governance architecture that enabled the systematic exclusion of Jewish students has enabled the exclusion of Women of Color from STEM departments with formal gender equity programs, of Black students from liberal arts colleges with explicit DEI commitments, of conservative faculty from universities claiming intellectual diversity, and of students with disabilities from institutions with compliance offices.

The cross-identity evidence is what confirms the structural diagnosis. At four public universities recognized as DEI leaders in computer science, researchers documented what they call "race-avoidant processes" — departmental governance that prioritized gender while systematically erasing race. Institutional DEI roles and departmental governance that narrowly define diversity operate as structural filters, protecting formal gender equity language while excluding Women of Color. At a predominantly White liberal arts college with explicit DEI commitments, a facially neutral behavioral pact produced systematic racial inequality through differential enforcement: Black students faced higher stakes for violations, reduced access to informal rule-breaking, and intensified surveillance. In legal academia, empirical research documents significant underrepresentation of conservative and libertarian scholars and identifies hiring processes in which committees systematically screen candidates based on ideological orientation — departments' emphasis on "fit" and networked recommendation channels functioning as exclusion mechanisms that no individual decision-maker needs to acknowledge.

In each case, formal institutional commitments to pluralism coexist with governance structures that predictably produce systematic exclusion. The architecture does not discriminate. It simply fails to constrain. The capturing faction need not be hostile — it needs only to be indifferent to the exclusion of a particular group, or willing to tolerate it as an acceptable cost of pursuing other goals.

> Governance reform that addresses the accountability void would benefit all affected constituencies, because the void is the disease and the exclusion is the symptom.

## The disentanglement problem

Part of what makes exclusive inclusion difficult to diagnose is that identity-based exclusion and legitimate institutional decisions often look identical from the outside. A department that declines to hire a conservative scholar may be exercising reasonable academic judgment or may be screening for ideological conformity. A student conduct office that disproportionately sanctions Black students may be applying rules evenhandedly or may be enforcing them through governance structures that produce systematically unequal outcomes.

I develop a diagnostic tool — the disentanglement matrix — to separate protected individual expression from institutional identity-based exclusion. The distinction matters because exclusive inclusion theory is agnostic on motivation. The governance analysis does not require determining whether individual actors harbor cultural animus, ideological commitments, or simple indifference. Many participants in the documented cases believed they were advancing legitimate institutional goals or expressing defensible political views. Whether they harbored hostility or indifference, the governance structure enabled systematic exclusion and afforded the excluded group no recourse. Structure shapes behavior more reliably than culture or individual attitudes.

## Why coercion hasn't worked

Between April and November 2025, the Trump administration froze billions in federal funding and conditioned restoration on sweeping changes to governance, speech policy, and admissions. Universities capitulated at speed. Columbia agreed to combined commitments of approximately $200 million. Brown settled for $50 million over ten years. Columbia separately faced cancellation of $400 million in federal grants and contracts.

Harvard fought back and won. The court found that "none of the federal government's grant termination letters specified how Harvard failed to respond to any acts of antisemitism in violation of Title VI" and concluded that the administration "used antisemitism as a smokescreen for a targeted, ideologically-motivated assault on this country's premier universities."

Yet the governance void that produced the original crisis persists at Harvard as at every other institution. The court's victory changed nothing about institutional architecture. Settlement agreements at other universities required adopting definitions, appointing liaisons, and conducting climate surveys. Not one settlement required governance reform. Not one mandated changes to board composition or selection procedures. Not one created stakeholder standing for students to challenge department-level exclusion.

The failure of federal coercion is not merely disappointing; it is structurally inevitable. No amount of federal pressure or policy reform can succeed when the governance architecture that produces exclusive inclusion remains unchanged. The settlements treated exclusive inclusion as a compliance deficit curable by policy adoption. The structural conditions producing exclusion remain unchanged.

## Three structural reforms

I propose three reforms that target each feature of the accountability void:

**Stakeholder standing** — modeled on corporate derivative suits — would allow affected students, faculty, or community members to challenge governance decisions that violate the institution's stated mission. The standing would be procedural, not substantive: it creates a mechanism for accountability, not a right to a particular outcome.

**Enforceable mission specificity** — universities would be required to state their commitments with enough precision that compliance is measurable and breach is adjudicable. Vague mission statements that promise everything and constrain nothing are the textual foundation of exclusive inclusion.

**An operational test for tax exemption** — adapted from hospital community benefit standards — would condition tax-exempt status on demonstrable governance performance, not merely stated intent.

Because exclusive inclusion is structural, governance reform benefits every affected constituency.
